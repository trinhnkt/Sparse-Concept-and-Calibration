\section{Experiments and Diagnostic Analysis}
\label{sec:experiments}

As mapped in Section~\ref{sec:introduction}, our experimental investigation is structured around three core Research Questions (RQs): RQ1 exercises Contribution 1, RQ2 exercises Contribution 2, and RQ3 draws jointly on Contributions 1 and 3.

\subsection{Experimental Setup and Datasets}
We evaluate our protocol on three diverse, public, de-identified benchmark educational datasets: ASSISTments 2012 (assist2012) \citep{assistments2012data,liu2022pykt}, Junyi Academy (junyi) \citep{junyi2019kaggle}, and XES3G5M (xes3g5m) \citep{liu2023xes3g5m}. No personally identifiable learner information is processed in this study. The preprocessing pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:protocol}) retains only well-formed interaction records; Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} presents the resulting cohort statistics.

\textbf{Dataset provenance.} ASSISTments 2012 was obtained from the ASSISTments 2012--2013 public release with affect predictions, as documented by ASSISTmentsData and the pyKT dataset documentation \citep{assistments2012data, liu2022pykt}. The Junyi Academy data used in this study corresponds to the Junyi Academy Online Learning Activity Dataset released on Kaggle \citep{junyi2019kaggle}, as reflected by the post-preprocessing cohort size in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} (71,014 learners, 25,784 items, 1,326 KCs, and 16.2M interactions); it should not be confused with the earlier Junyi2015 release, which has different learner/item/KC counts. XES3G5M was obtained from the benchmark release of \citet{liu2023xes3g5m}. All counts in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} are post-preprocessing counts produced by our normalization pipeline. Consequently, the number of KCs appearing in bucket-level temporal tables can be smaller than the dataset-level \#KCs in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} when some KCs have no valid evaluated prediction rows after temporal filtering and sequence construction; we report these evaluated-KC totals explicitly below Table~\ref{tab:temporal_strata}.

Learner-based splits serve as the main predictive evaluation setting (reported in Tables~\ref{tab:overall_learner} and~\ref{tab:strata_learner}, \ifdefined\iscompact detailed calibration results are provided in Supplementary Table S2\else with detailed calibration results in Appendix~\ref{app:calibration_performance}, Table~\ref{tab:calibration_learner}\fi), because they test generalization to unseen learners while preserving enough interactions for stable bucket-level analysis. Temporal splits serve as a complementary stress test for future-oriented and cold-start diagnostics (reported in Table~\ref{tab:cold_start_temporal} and \ifdefined\iscompact Supplementary Table S3\else Appendix~\ref{app:temporal_performance}\fi). Keeping the two modes separate prevents learner-level and curriculum-level forecasting challenges from being conflated.

For all deep baselines on learner-based splits, we report mean $\pm$ standard deviation across five evaluation runs (folds corresponding to seeds 42, 2024, 2025, 2026, 2027). For temporal splits, after the label-alignment correction documented in Appendix~\ref{app:alignment}, we report deterministic metrics from the single corrected evaluation run (seed 42) used to regenerate Figure~\ref{fig:reliability_junyi_temporal} and the temporal calibration tables. This single-run design is intentional but limited: unlike learner-based cross-validation, which randomly partitions students, the temporal split relies on a single deterministic chronological boundary (an 80/20 time cutoff) to simulate future forecasting. Because the partition is fixed, the reported temporal results constitute a fixed-cutoff stress test; they do not estimate variance across temporal cut-points or neural initializations. We do not claim that initialization variance is absent, and we return to this limitation in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}. IRT is deterministic given a fold, and its learner-based variation reflects fold differences only.

\subsection{Baseline Implementations}
We initially evaluated BKT but replaced it with IRT after observing numerical instability in EM-based \texttt{pyBKT} estimation; details are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:bkt_instability}. We evaluate three representative classes of KT models:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Item Response Theory (IRT 1PL):} a classical logistic baseline (one-parameter logistic Rasch model) estimating a static learner ability and concept difficulty from training interactions. Because the model relies on learner identities, unseen learners in learner-based splits have no estimated ability parameters; in our implementation the prediction then falls back to a global, base-rate-like probability. Under learner-based splits we therefore treat IRT as a base-rate reference rather than as a discriminative baseline ($\text{AUC} = 0.50$ by construction). Under temporal splits, where learners are observed during training, IRT functions as a genuine classical baseline and serves as the static reference for audit channel L8 (Appendix~\ref{app:alignment}).
    \item \textbf{Deep Knowledge Tracing (DKT):} The pioneering recurrent neural network KT architecture mapping student sequential states via LSTM/RNN cells.
    \item \textbf{SimpleKT:} A modern, self-attention-based model utilizing key-query similarities between concept embeddings to track mastery.
\end{enumerate}

All deep learning models are trained with validation-based early stopping to prevent hyperparameter leakage.

\subsection{RQ1: Stratum-level Predictive Heterogeneity}
To address RQ1, we first inspect the overall performance of all three baselines in Table~\ref{tab:overall_learner}. SimpleKT and DKT achieve high aggregate AUC scores under learner-based splits, with DKT slightly leading SimpleKT on ASSISTments 2012 ($0.6980 \pm 0.0013$ vs. $0.6840 \pm 0.0025$). As discussed, IRT performs at random (AUC $= 0.5000$) on learner-based splits due to its base-rate-like prediction for unseen learners. Under temporal splits (Table~\ref{tab:temporal_overall} in Appendix~\ref{app:temporal_performance}), where learners are observed, IRT's AUC rises to $0.5913$ on ASSISTments 2012, while SimpleKT ($0.6731$) slightly outperforms DKT ($0.6606$).

Overall AUC differences between DKT and SimpleKT are statistically significant on all three datasets after Bonferroni correction, confirming that aggregate predictive quality differs between the two deep baselines.

The stratum-level breakdown in Table~\ref{tab:strata_learner} reveals a heterogeneous pattern rather than a simple monotonic AUC degradation. In several cases, medium or sparse strata even show higher AUC than dense strata; however, calibration, NLL/RMSE, and sample-size stability can still deteriorate. This supports the central point that aggregate AUC alone is insufficient for diagnosing KT reliability. Therefore, RQ1 should not be interpreted as proving universal AUC degradation on sparse KCs, but as showing that aggregate metrics can hide heterogeneous stratum-level behavior.

The evaluated-KC totals are reported in the note below Table~\ref{tab:strata_learner} to clarify that bucket-level analyses cover only KCs with valid evaluated prediction rows.

We note that the behavior observed in sparse and very sparse strata represents a combination of true performance differences and metric instability. For instance, very sparse strata can yield unstable estimates when the number of test events is small. Therefore, bucket-level diagnostics must be interpreted jointly with sample size, as formalized by our reliability flags.

An instructive counter-pattern emerges on XES3G5M. Even after separating strict cold-start KCs from the very sparse stratum, DKT and SimpleKT show higher AUC on sparse KCs than on dense KCs (DKT: 0.8547 vs. 0.8168; SimpleKT: 0.8455 vs. 0.7548), and the very sparse stratum remains competitive for both (DKT: 0.8172; SimpleKT: 0.8188), subject to its Limited flag. Strict cold-start rows are reported for completeness only. Several non-exclusive explanations are plausible: (i) XES3G5M's hierarchical KC tree may place sparse KCs among advanced concepts reached only by learners who have mastered prerequisites, creating a ceiling effect; (ii) item-level features may carry more signal than KC-level features, partially decoupling KC frequency from predictive difficulty; and (iii) multi-skill expansion may concentrate predictions on items whose primary skill is well trained even when the auxiliary skill is sparse. Disambiguating these explanations requires item-level error analysis beyond the scope of this protocol paper, but the pattern itself reinforces the central point: bucket-level diagnostics reveal phenomena that overall AUC cannot.

\subsection{RQ2: Calibration Profiles by Frequency}
To answer RQ2, we analyze Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and Brier Score decomposition across frequency strata. On Junyi Academy under the learner-based split, the sparse and very sparse buckets are empty because all KCs have training-fold frequency at least 100. This absence is itself a diagnostic observation: Junyi does not exhibit learner-based sparse-KC concerns under the pre-registered thresholds.

Detailed calibration results are reported in \ifdefined\iscompact Supplementary Table S2\else Appendix~\ref{app:calibration_performance}, Table~\ref{tab:calibration_learner}\fi.

Notably, IRT exhibits very low ECE on several dense strata (e.g., $0.0033$ on ASSISTments 2012) yet has zero Brier resolution (RES $= 0$) across strata and random discrimination (AUC $= 0.50$) on learner-based splits. This occurs because IRT lacks ability estimates for unseen learners and predicts close to the global correctness rate, yielding low reliability error (REL $\approx 0$) but no discriminative power. This illustrates a critical point: a model can appear well-calibrated precisely by being uninformative.

\textit{Calibration gradient on ASSISTments 2012.}
Under our experimental conditions, ASSISTments 2012 provides the clearest example of the calibration gradient the protocol targets. For SimpleKT, ECE increases from $0.1131 \pm 0.0076$ (dense) to $0.1578$ (medium) and $0.2254 \pm 0.0204$ (sparse), and reaches $0.2464 \pm 0.0679$ in the very sparse stratum; the sparse and very sparse figures carry Limited and Insufficient flags, respectively, and are interpreted accordingly. A similar monotonic pattern appears in DKT's reliability component, with REL rising from $0.0053$ (dense) to $0.0316$ (medium) and $0.0623$ (sparse). Since lower ECE and lower REL indicate better calibration, probability reliability deteriorates as KC training evidence decreases, even while aggregate predictive metrics remain competitive. On the same dense stratum, IRT reports far lower ECE ($0.0033$) than DKT ($0.0602$) and SimpleKT ($0.1131$), but per the base-rate analysis above this does not indicate superior predictive reliability: IRT has $\text{RES} = 0$ and $\text{AUC} = 0.50$ there.

Because temporal splits stress future-concept generalization, we use the Junyi temporal split in Figure~\ref{fig:reliability_junyi_temporal} to visualize calibration behavior. The dense stratum has a lower ECE (0.0889), whereas the sparse stratum shows higher ECE (0.1624) while retaining meaningful discrimination (AUC = 0.6529). This comparison highlights why calibration and discrimination must be interpreted jointly.

\subsection{RQ3: Limited Cold-start Concept Diagnostics}
How do baseline KT models behave on KCs with zero or limited training-fold frequency under temporal splits? We address this by evaluating models on limited cold-start concepts (strict, k5, k10, warm) under temporal splitting in Table~\ref{tab:cold_start_temporal}.

\input{tables/table_06_cold_start_temporal_validation}
\FloatBarrier

Temporal splits expose a demanding generalization setting. Both DKT and SimpleKT generalize poorly to KCs with zero or limited training-fold history, yielding near-random performance (AUC $\approx 0.41$--$0.51$) on strict and limited cold-start groups (e.g., DKT $0.5055$ and SimpleKT $0.4318$ on ASSISTments 2012 strict cold-start)---expected, since the models lack parameter history for unseen concepts. After the label-alignment correction of Appendix~\ref{app:alignment} was applied consistently across datasets, the deep baselines recover meaningful signal on warm temporal cohorts: DKT and SimpleKT reach $0.6949$ and $0.7167$ on Junyi Academy, $0.6626$ and $0.6615$ on XES3G5M, and $0.6606$ and $0.6734$ on ASSISTments 2012. The contrast is the diagnostic payoff: the dominant temporal difficulty lies in generalizing to unseen or low-frequency KCs, not in forecasting familiar ones, and the warm-cohort recovery is what distinguishes genuine concept-level cold-start failure from pipeline-induced random predictions (audit channel L8).
