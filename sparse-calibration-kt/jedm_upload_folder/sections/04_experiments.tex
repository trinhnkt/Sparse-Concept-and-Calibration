\section{Experiments and Diagnostic Analysis}
\label{sec:experiments}

As mapped in Section~\ref{sec:introduction}, our experimental investigation is structured around three core Research Questions (RQs): RQ1 exercises Contribution 1, RQ2 exercises Contribution 2, and RQ3 draws jointly on Contributions 1 and 3.

\subsection{Experimental Setup and Datasets}
We evaluate our protocol on three diverse, public, de-identified benchmark educational datasets: ASSISTments 2012 (\path{assist2012}) \citep{assistments2012}, Junyi Academy (\path{junyi}) \citep{junyi2019kaggle}, and XES3G5M (\path{xes3g5m}) \citep{liu2023xes3g5m}. No personally identifiable information (PII) is processed. The preprocessing and filtering pipeline (Section~\ref{sec:protocol}) ensures that only high-quality interaction records are retained. Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} presents the final statistics of each dataset cohort.

\textbf{Dataset provenance.} ASSISTments 2012 was obtained from the ASSISTments 2012--2013 public release \citep{assistments2012}. The Junyi Academy data used in this study corresponds to the Kaggle 2018--2019 dataset release \citep{junyi2019kaggle}, as reflected by the post-preprocessing cohort size in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} (71,014 learners, 25,784 items, 1,326 KCs, and 16.2M interactions); it should not be confused with the earlier Junyi2015 release, which has different learner/item/KC counts. XES3G5M was obtained from the benchmark release of \citet{liu2023xes3g5m}. All counts in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} are post-preprocessing counts produced by our normalization pipeline. Consequently, the number of KCs appearing in bucket-level temporal tables can be smaller than the dataset-level \#KCs in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats} when some KCs have no valid evaluated prediction rows after temporal filtering and sequence construction; we report these evaluated-KC totals explicitly below Table~\ref{tab:temporal_strata}.

We use learner-based splits as the main predictive evaluation setting (reported in Tables~\ref{tab:overall_learner} and~\ref{tab:strata_learner}, \ifdefined\iscompact detailed calibration results are provided in Supplementary Table S2\else with detailed calibration results in Appendix~\ref{app:calibration_performance}, Table~\ref{tab:calibration_learner}\fi) because they test generalization to unseen learners while preserving enough interactions for stable bucket-level analysis. Temporal splits are used as a complementary stress test for future-oriented and limited cold-start diagnostics, reported in Table~\ref{tab:cold_start_temporal} and \ifdefined\iscompact Supplementary Table S3\else Appendix~\ref{app:temporal_performance}\fi. This explicit partitioning ensures that evaluation pipelines do not conflate learner-level and curriculum-level forecasting challenges.

We report mean $\pm$ standard deviation across five evaluation runs for all deep baselines on learner-based splits (folds corresponding to seeds 42, 2024, 2025, 2026, 2027). For temporal splits, after the label-alignment correction documented in Appendix~\ref{app:alignment}, we report deterministic metrics from the single corrected evaluation run (seed 42) used to regenerate Figure~\ref{fig:reliability_junyi_temporal} and the temporal calibration tables. This single-run design is intentional: unlike learner-based cross-validation which randomly partitions students, temporal splitting relies on a single, deterministic chronological boundary (e.g., an 80/20 time cutoff) to simulate real-world future forecasting. Because the temporal partition is a fixed-cutoff stress test, the reported temporal results focus on this fixed evaluation rather than estimating variance across temporal cut-points or neural initializations. Extending the temporal diagnostics to multiple seeds or cut-points is left for future work. Additionally, the \#Events column denotes the number of prediction rows used for metric computation after model-specific prediction export and KC-strata matching. Following design principle P3, we annotate each bucket-level result with a reliability flag based on its sample size.

\subsection{Baseline Implementations}
We initially evaluated BKT but replaced it with IRT after observing numerical instability in EM-based \texttt{pyBKT} estimation; details are provided in Appendix~\ref{app:bkt_instability}. We evaluate three representative classes of KT models:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Item Response Theory (IRT 1PL):} A classical logistic baseline that estimates a static user ability and concept difficulty. Under learner-based splits, IRT is a base-rate reference, not a discriminative baseline. Because unseen learners have no estimated ability parameters, prediction falls back to a global/base-rate-like probability. However, under temporal splits where learners are observed, IRT acts as a genuine classical baseline and a static reference for the L8 audit check.
    \item \textbf{Deep Knowledge Tracing (DKT):} The pioneering recurrent neural network KT architecture mapping student sequential states via LSTM/RNN cells.
    \item \textbf{SimpleKT:} A modern, self-attention-based model utilizing key-query similarities between concept embeddings to track mastery.
\end{enumerate}

All deep learning models are trained with validation-based early stopping to prevent hyperparameter leakage.

\subsection{RQ1: Predictive Strata Degradation}
To address RQ1, we first inspect the overall performance of all three baselines in Table~\ref{tab:overall_learner}. SimpleKT and DKT achieve high aggregate AUC scores under learner-based splits, with DKT slightly leading SimpleKT on ASSISTments 2012 ($0.6980 \pm 0.0013$ vs. $0.6840 \pm 0.0025$). As discussed, IRT performs at random (AUC $= 0.5000$) on learner-based splits due to its base-rate-like prediction for unseen learners. Under temporal splits (Table~\ref{tab:temporal_overall} in Appendix~\ref{app:temporal_performance}), where learners are observed, IRT's AUC rises to $0.5913$ on ASSISTments 2012, while SimpleKT ($0.6731$) slightly outperforms DKT ($0.6606$).

Overall AUC differences between DKT and SimpleKT are statistically significant on all three datasets after Bonferroni correction, confirming that aggregate predictive quality differs between the two deep baselines.

The stratum-level breakdown in Table~\ref{tab:strata_learner} reveals a more nuanced picture. Rather than showing a simple monotonic AUC degradation pattern, the medium and sparse strata exhibit heterogeneous behavior: in some cases AUC is higher than in dense strata, but calibration, NLL/RMSE, or sample-size stability can deteriorate. This supports the central point that aggregate AUC alone is insufficient for diagnosing KT reliability.

\input{tables/table5_bucket_performance}

After test-fold filtering and KC-strata matching, the evaluated-KC totals under learner-based splits were 260/265 for ASSISTments 2012, 1,326/1,326 for Junyi Academy, and 834/866 for XES3G5M. We explicitly note two minor numerical discrepancies that are retained transparently as artifacts of the underlying evaluation pipeline rather than being artificially smoothed. First, under the Junyi Academy temporal split, the evaluated KC total drops slightly to 1,323 (compared to the 1,326 total KCs reported in Table~\ref{tab:dataset_stats}), because three concepts present in the dataset lacked valid test-fold predictions after temporal sequence-length filtering. Second, minor discrepancies in the number of evaluated events between models (e.g., Junyi dense events for DKT vs. SimpleKT) arise because different sequence-unrolling and padding mechanisms can drop disparate numbers of trailing interactions. We report the exact sample sizes corresponding to the evaluated predictions.

We note that the behavior observed in sparse and very sparse strata represents a combination of true performance differences and metric instability. For instance, very sparse strata can yield unstable estimates when the number of test events is small. Therefore, bucket-level diagnostics must be interpreted jointly with sample size, as formalized by our reliability flags.

An interesting counter-pattern emerges on XES3G5M: both DKT and SimpleKT show higher AUC on sparse KCs than on dense KCs (e.g., DKT: 0.8547 vs. 0.8168). The very sparse stratum remains competitive, but it should be interpreted cautiously because of its Limited reliability flag. Strict cold-start rows are reported for completeness only. Several non-exclusive mechanisms may explain this counter-pattern. For example, XES3G5M's hierarchical KC tree structure may mean that sparse KCs are advanced concepts reached only by learners who have mastered prerequisites, creating a ceiling effect on accuracy. Disambiguating these possible mechanisms requires item-level analysis beyond the scope of this protocol paper, but the pattern reinforces our point that bucket-level diagnostics reveal phenomena that overall AUC alone cannot.

\subsection{RQ2: Calibration Profiles by Frequency}
To answer RQ2, we analyze Expected Calibration Error (ECE) and Brier Score decomposition across frequency strata. On Junyi Academy under the learner-based split, the sparse and very sparse buckets are empty because all KCs have training-fold frequency at least 100. This absence is itself a diagnostic observation: Junyi does not exhibit learner-based sparse-KC concerns under the pre-registered thresholds.

Detailed calibration results are reported in \ifdefined\iscompact Supplementary Table S2\else Appendix~\ref{app:calibration_performance}, Table~\ref{tab:calibration_learner}\fi.

Notably, IRT exhibits very low ECE on several dense strata (e.g., $0.0033$ on ASSISTments 2012) yet has zero Brier resolution (RES $= 0$) across strata and random discrimination (AUC $= 0.50$) on learner-based splits. This occurs because IRT lacks ability estimates for unseen learners and predicts close to the global correctness rate, yielding low reliability error (REL $\approx 0$) but no discriminative power. This illustrates a critical point: a model can appear well-calibrated precisely by being uninformative.

\textit{Calibration gradient on ASSISTments 2012.}
Under our experimental conditions, ASSISTments 2012 provides the clearest example of the calibration gradient. For SimpleKT, ECE increases from $0.1131$ in the dense stratum to $0.1578$ in the medium stratum, $0.2254$ in the sparse stratum, and $0.2464$ in the very sparse stratum. A similar degradation appears for DKT, where the reliability component (REL) increases from $0.0053$ (dense) to $0.0316$ (medium) and $0.0623$ (sparse). Since lower ECE and REL indicate better calibration, these results show that probability reliability can deteriorate as KC training evidence decreases, even when aggregate predictive metrics remain competitive. Sparse and very sparse strata must be interpreted in conjunction with their reliability flags.

Because temporal splits stress future-concept generalization, we use the Junyi temporal split in Figure~\ref{fig:reliability_junyi_temporal} to visualize calibration behavior. The dense stratum has a lower ECE (0.0889), whereas the sparse stratum shows higher ECE (0.1624) while retaining meaningful discrimination (AUC = 0.6529). This comparison highlights why calibration and discrimination must be interpreted jointly.

\subsection{RQ3: Limited Cold-start Concept Diagnostics}
How do baseline KT models behave on KCs with zero or limited training-fold frequency under temporal splits? We address this by evaluating models on limited cold-start concepts (strict, k5, k10, warm) under temporal splitting in Table~\ref{tab:cold_start_temporal}.

Under our experimental conditions, temporal splits expose a challenging generalization setting: both DKT and SimpleKT show near-random predictive performance (AUC $\approx$ 0.41--0.51) on strict and limited cold-start groups (e.g., k5, k10). However, the warm cohort recovers meaningful signal after the label-alignment correction documented in Appendix~\ref{app:alignment}. For example, on Junyi Academy, DKT and SimpleKT reach warm-cohort AUCs of $0.6949$ and $0.7167$, respectively. This pattern indicates that the main temporal difficulty lies in generalizing to unseen or low-frequency KCs rather than in forecasting familiar KCs. In this context, the L8 channel distinguishes genuine concept-level cold-start failure from pipeline-induced random predictions.
